A.Li.V.E. —
Air Quality Decision Tool

An air quality decision tool for the London Borough of Enfield — visualising the impact of three pollution-reduction schemes across the borough's road network.

Challenge

The London Borough of Enfield needed to evaluate three competing schemes for reducing air pollution — increasing cycling infrastructure, expanding low-emission vehicles, and improving traffic flow around schools. Decision-makers lacked a way to compare the projected impact of each scheme against budget constraints and time horizons.

Process

Working with a multidisciplinary team, I designed the interface layer that made the underlying pollution data model legible and interactive. A crossfilter mechanism let decision-makers layer the three schemes simultaneously; a budget toggle showed the gap between current and invested pollution levels; a time picker let users scrub through daily pollution patterns across the Enfield road network.

Outcome

An interactive decision tool — A.Li.V.E. (Air for Life Visualization in Enfield) — that allowed civic planners to compare pollution reduction scenarios, balance budget trade-offs, and understand the spatial distribution of air quality improvements across the borough. Built for tablet use in stakeholder presentations and planning sessions.

01 — The interface

Seven layers, one view

The A.Li.V.E. interface is built around a single split-screen map that renders two pollution scenarios simultaneously. Seven interactive controls govern what the user sees: pollution maps, pollution level, a budget toggle, colour mixer, crossfilter scheme selectors, map layers, and a draggable time picker. All of them update the map in real time.

02 — Comparing scenarios

Side by side

The core interaction: two scenarios rendered side by side on the Enfield road map, with pollution levels shown as colour-coded spatial blobs. Pink indicates high pollution; teal indicates lower pollution. Scenario 1 (bicycle scheme, 43 µg/m³) vs Scenario 2 (low-emission vehicles, 65 µg/m³) — the visual difference is immediate.

03 — Interactions

Time & sensor drill-down

Users can filter the data by a specific date and time using the calendar and time picker — then click into any individual sensor node on the map to see a historical pollution graph for that location. This drill-down from borough-wide overview to sensor-level detail was central to how planners used the tool in stakeholder sessions.

04 — In context

Exhibition display

A.Li.V.E. was demoed on a large curved display, allowing multiple stakeholders to interact with the interface, view the scenarios simultaneously, and point to specific areas of the map during discussion. The interface held up at scale — the spatial data model and the colour system were legible at 3 metres.
