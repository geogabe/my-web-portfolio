Less Than a Second
in Formula 1

A data visualisation poster for the British Grand Prix — exploring the margins that separate winners from the field in the world's fastest motorsport.

Challenge

Formula 1 race data is extraordinarily dense — lap times measured in thousandths of seconds, tyre degradation curves, sector-level competitor splits. The challenge was to transform raw telemetry from the 2013 GB Grand Prix into a compelling visualisation that could engage a general audience while doing justice to the complexity of the sport.

Process

Working in a team of three, I led the full visual treatment. A data analyst crunched the race dataset; a second designer handled the narrative structure; I owned the visual language, typography, colour system, and the final rendered compositions. The central circular form encodes every lap of the race — driver positions, pit windows, and tyre events — in a single readable glyph.

Outcome

A data visualisation poster that makes the marginal differences in F1 visible and emotionally legible — showing how less than a second per lap across a race distance separates the front of the grid from the midfield. The visual system was built to work at large exhibition scale while remaining readable close up.

01 — The poster

Race visualised

The full GB Grand Prix 2013 poster. Every lap of the race is encoded in the central spiral — driver positions are colour-coded lines, tyre changes appear as breaks in the arc, and Hamilton's tyre failure on lap 7 is visible as a sharp deviation in the data. The bottom section compares a F1 car against a road car across key performance dimensions.

02 — The data structure

How the spiral works

An exploded view of the circular race chart — showing how the data layers are constructed. The outer ring tracks all 11 drivers; the inner spiral is the reference driver (Hamilton); the delta spiral shows how much faster or slower each driver is relative to the reference, lap by lap. The grid encodes lap number and time delta simultaneously.

03 — The detail

Reading the race

A close-up of the GB Grand Prix spiral annotated for readability — showing how the chart encodes Rosberg running 20 seconds ahead of Hamilton before his pit stop, then losing 9 seconds in the stop itself, and how the brightness of each line communicates tyre degradation across the stint.
